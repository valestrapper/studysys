01 NUMBER SYSTEMS▶

Types of Numbers

  • Natural numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4... (counting)
  • Whole numbers: 0, 1, 2, 3... (natural + zero)
  • Integers: ...-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3... (pos, neg, zero)
  • Rational: any fraction a/b where b≠0 (includes terminating and repeating decimals)
  • Irrational: cannot be written as fraction — decimal never ends or repeats (π, √2, √3, e)
  • Real numbers: all rational AND irrational combined

Integer Operations

  • Same signs when multiplying/dividing → POSITIVE
  • Different signs → NEGATIVE
  • Adding same sign: add and keep the sign
  • Adding different signs: subtract, keep sign of larger absolute value
  • Subtracting: add the opposite (a - b = a + (-b))
  • Double negative: -(-5) = +5

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

  • P — Parentheses (innermost first)
  • E — Exponents (powers and roots)
  • M/D — Multiply & Divide (left to right, same priority)
  • A/S — Add & Subtract (left to right, same priority)
  • Example: 3 + 4×2² = 3 + 4×4 = 3 + 16 = 19
  • Brackets work same as parentheses

Absolute Value

  • |x| = distance from zero on number line
  • Always ≥ 0, never negative
  • |-7| = 7, |7| = 7, |0| = 0
  • |a| = |-a| for any a
  • Used for: distance, error, temperature difference

Prime & Composite Numbers

  • Prime: exactly 2 factors (1 and itself) — 2,3,5,7,11,13,17,19,23...
  • Composite: more than 2 factors — 4,6,8,9,10,12...
  • 1 is NEITHER prime nor composite
  • 2 is the ONLY even prime number
  • GCF: greatest common factor of two numbers
  • LCM: least common multiple of two numbers

Divisibility Rules

  • ÷2: ends in 0,2,4,6,8
  • ÷3: digit sum divisible by 3
  • ÷4: last two digits divisible by 4
  • ÷5: ends in 0 or 5
  • ÷6: divisible by both 2 AND 3
  • ÷9: digit sum divisible by 9
  • ÷10: ends in 0
02 FRACTIONS, DECIMALS & PERCENTS▶

Fraction Operations

  • ADD/SUB: find LCD, convert, then add/subtract numerators
  • MULTIPLY: multiply tops × tops, bottoms × bottoms — simplify first if possible
  • DIVIDE: Keep first, Change ÷ to ×, Flip second (KCF)
  • Mixed → improper: (whole × denom + num) / denom
  • Improper → mixed: divide, remainder becomes new numerator
  • Simplify: divide both by GCF

Decimal Conversions

  • Fraction → decimal: divide numerator by denominator
  • Decimal → fraction: write over place value, simplify
  • Terminating decimal: division ends (1/4 = 0.25)
  • Repeating decimal: pattern repeats (1/3 = 0.333...)
  • % → decimal: divide by 100 (shift left 2)
  • Decimal → %: multiply by 100 (shift right 2)

Percent Problems

  • % of number: convert to decimal, multiply
  • What % is part of whole? (part/whole) × 100
  • Finding whole: part ÷ percent (as decimal)
  • % increase: (new−old)/old × 100
  • % decrease: (old−new)/old × 100
  • Tax: original × tax rate; add to original
  • Tip: original × tip rate; add to original
  • Discount: original × discount rate; subtract from original

Ratios & Proportions

  • Ratio: comparison a:b or a/b
  • Proportion: two equal ratios a/b = c/d
  • Cross multiply to solve: ad = bc
  • Unit rate: per-one comparison (miles per hour)
  • Scale factor: ratio of similar figure dimensions
  • Constant of proportionality: k in y = kx
Simple Interest: I = Prt (P=principal, r=rate as decimal, t=time in years)
Compound Interest: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) — interest calculated on accumulated interest
Sale price: P × (1 − discount%)  |  After tax: P × (1 + tax%)
03 VARIABLES, EXPRESSIONS & EQUATIONS▶

Algebraic Vocabulary

  • Variable: letter representing unknown quantity
  • Constant: fixed number that doesn't change
  • Coefficient: number multiplied by a variable
  • Term: number, variable, or product of both
  • Expression: terms combined (no = sign)
  • Equation: expression = expression (has = sign)
  • Like terms: same variable AND same exponent

Combining Like Terms

  • Only add/subtract terms with identical variables and exponents
  • Add/subtract the coefficients ONLY
  • 3x + 5x = 8x ✓
  • 3x + 5y = 3x + 5y (can't combine) ✗
  • 7x² − 3x² = 4x²
  • Constants combine with constants: 5 + 3 = 8

Distributive Property

  • a(b + c) = ab + ac — multiply EVERY term inside
  • a(b − c) = ab − ac
  • 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12
  • -2(x − 5) = -2x + 10 (negatives flip signs)
  • -(x + 3) = -x − 3 (distribute the negative)
  • Use to remove parentheses before combining like terms

Solving One-Step Equations

  • Goal: isolate the variable on one side
  • Use inverse (opposite) operations
  • Whatever you do to one side, MUST do to both
  • x + 7 = 15 → subtract 7 → x = 8
  • x − 4 = 9 → add 4 → x = 13
  • 3x = 18 → divide 3 → x = 6
  • x/5 = 4 → multiply 5 → x = 20

Solving Multi-Step Equations

  • Step 1: Distribute (remove parentheses)
  • Step 2: Combine like terms on each side
  • Step 3: Move variables to one side
  • Step 4: Undo addition/subtraction
  • Step 5: Undo multiplication/division
  • Always CHECK by substituting answer back in

Special Equation Situations

  • Variables cancel, TRUE statement (2=2): infinitely many solutions
  • Variables cancel, FALSE statement (3=5): no solution
  • Literal equations: solve for one variable in terms of others
  • Example: d = rt → solve for t: t = d/r
04 INEQUALITIES & GRAPHING ON NUMBER LINE▶

Inequality Symbols

  • < less than — open circle, shade left
  • > greater than — open circle, shade right
  • ≤ less than or EQUAL — closed circle, shade left
  • ≥ greater than or EQUAL — closed circle, shade right
  • ≠ not equal to

Solving Inequalities

  • Same steps as solving equations
  • KEY RULE: FLIP the inequality sign when multiplying OR dividing by a NEGATIVE number
  • −2x < 8 → divide by −2 → x > −4 (flipped!)
  • Adding/subtracting does NOT flip sign
  • Multiplying/dividing by positive does NOT flip

Compound Inequalities

  • AND (conjunction): both conditions true; "between" — graph is overlap
  • OR (disjunction): either condition true — graph is union
  • -3 < x < 5 means x > -3 AND x < 5
  • Solve each part separately, then combine on graph
05 GEOMETRY — ANGLES, SHAPES & MEASUREMENT▶

Angle Relationships

  • Complementary: two angles that add to 90°
  • Supplementary: two angles that add to 180°
  • Vertical angles: across from each other at intersection; ALWAYS equal
  • Adjacent angles: share a side and vertex
  • Linear pair: adjacent angles on a straight line; sum = 180°
  • Triangle angles: always sum to 180°
  • Quadrilateral angles: always sum to 360°
  • Exterior angle of triangle = sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles

Area Formulas

  • Rectangle: A = l × w
  • Square: A = s²
  • Triangle: A = ½bh
  • Parallelogram: A = bh (h is perpendicular height!)
  • Trapezoid: A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h
  • Circle: A = πr²
  • Composite: break into smaller shapes, add/subtract

Perimeter & Circumference

  • Rectangle: P = 2l + 2w = 2(l+w)
  • Square: P = 4s
  • Triangle: add all three sides
  • Circle circumference: C = 2πr = πd
  • π ≈ 3.14159 (use 3.14 unless told otherwise)
  • Arc length: (θ/360) × 2πr

Surface Area & Volume

  • Rectangular prism: V = lwh, SA = 2(lw+lh+wh)
  • Cube: V = s³, SA = 6s²
  • Cylinder: V = πr²h, SA = 2πr² + 2πrh
  • Cone: V = ⅓πr²h
  • Pyramid: V = ⅓Bh (B = base area)
  • Sphere: V = (4/3)πr³, SA = 4πr²

Pythagorean Theorem

  • For RIGHT triangles ONLY: a² + b² = c²
  • c is ALWAYS the hypotenuse (longest side, opposite right angle)
  • a and b are the two legs
  • Finding hypotenuse: c = √(a²+b²)
  • Finding a leg: a = √(c²−b²)
  • Common triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17

Transformations

  • Translation: slide (add/subtract to coordinates)
  • Reflection: flip over a line (x-axis flips y sign, y-axis flips x sign)
  • Rotation: turn around a point
  • Dilation: resize by scale factor k
  • Rigid transformations: keep shape AND size (translation, reflection, rotation)
  • Congruent: same shape and size; Similar: same shape, different size
06 EXPONENTS & SCIENTIFIC NOTATION▶

Exponent Rules

  • Product rule: xᵃ × xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ (same base, ADD exponents)
  • Quotient rule: xᵃ ÷ xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ (same base, SUBTRACT)
  • Power rule: (xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ (multiply exponents)
  • Zero exponent: x⁰ = 1 (any nonzero base to zero power)
  • Negative exponent: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ (flip to denominator)
  • Product to a power: (xy)ᵃ = xᵃyᵃ

Scientific Notation

  • Form: a × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ a < 10
  • Large numbers: positive exponent (6.02 × 10²³)
  • Small numbers: negative exponent (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)
  • Converting: count decimal places moved
  • Multiply: multiply the a's, ADD the exponents
  • Divide: divide the a's, SUBTRACT the exponents

Square & Cube Roots

  • √x = number that squares to x
  • Perfect squares: 1,4,9,16,25,36,49,64,81,100,121,144,169,196,225
  • Cube root: ∛x = number that cubes to x
  • Perfect cubes: 1,8,27,64,125,216,343,512
  • Irrational roots: simplify by factoring out perfect squares
  • √12 = √(4×3) = 2√3
07 STATISTICS & PROBABILITY▶

Measures of Center

  • Mean: sum of all values ÷ count (average)
  • Median: middle value when ordered; if even count, average the two middle
  • Mode: most frequently occurring value (can have none or multiple)
  • Range: max − min (spread, not center)
  • Outliers affect the mean most; median is more resistant

Data Displays

  • Stem-and-leaf: shows actual data values
  • Box plot (box-and-whisker): min, Q1, median, Q3, max
  • Histogram: bars for ranges (no gaps), shows frequency
  • Scatter plot: shows relationship between two variables
  • Line graph: change over time
  • Bar graph: compare categories

Probability

  • P(event) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes
  • Always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain)
  • Complement: P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
  • Theoretical vs experimental probability
  • Independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
  • Dependent events: second event affected by first
  • Mutually exclusive: can't happen at same time; P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

Counting & Combinations

  • Fundamental Counting Principle: multiply choices at each step
  • Permutation: order MATTERS (nPr)
  • Combination: order does NOT matter (nCr)
  • Tree diagrams: show all possible outcomes
  • Sample space: set of all possible outcomes
08 PROPORTIONAL RELATIONSHIPS & FUNCTIONS▶

Proportional Relationships

  • y = kx where k is the constant of proportionality
  • Graph: straight line through the ORIGIN (0,0)
  • k = y/x (unit rate)
  • Table: y/x ratio is always the same
  • Non-proportional: line doesn't pass through origin

Rate of Change & Slope

  • Rate of change = change in y / change in x
  • Slope m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁) = rise/run
  • Positive slope: line goes up left to right
  • Negative slope: line goes down left to right
  • Zero slope: horizontal line
  • Undefined slope: vertical line

Linear vs Non-Linear

  • Linear: constant rate of change, graph is straight line
  • Non-linear: rate of change varies, curved graph
  • Table check for linear: equal x-differences → equal y-differences
  • Direct variation: y = kx passes through origin

01 PROPERTIES OF REAL NUMBERS▶

Number Properties

  • Commutative (Add): a + b = b + a
  • Commutative (Mult): a × b = b × a
  • Associative (Add): (a+b)+c = a+(b+c)
  • Associative (Mult): (a×b)×c = a×(b×c)
  • Distributive: a(b+c) = ab + ac
  • Identity (Add): a + 0 = a
  • Identity (Mult): a × 1 = a
  • Inverse (Add): a + (−a) = 0
  • Inverse (Mult): a × (1/a) = 1 (a≠0)
  • Zero Property: a × 0 = 0

Exponent Rules (Complete)

  • xᵃ × xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ (product rule)
  • xᵃ ÷ xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ (quotient rule)
  • (xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ (power of a power)
  • (xy)ᵃ = xᵃyᵃ (power of a product)
  • (x/y)ᵃ = xᵃ/yᵃ (power of a quotient)
  • x⁰ = 1, x ≠ 0
  • x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ (negative exponents)

Scientific Notation Operations

  • Form: a × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ a < 10
  • Multiply: (a × 10ⁿ)(b × 10ᵐ) = ab × 10ⁿ⁺ᵐ
  • Divide: (a × 10ⁿ)/(b × 10ᵐ) = (a/b) × 10ⁿ⁻ᵐ
  • After operating, re-adjust so a is between 1-10
  • Adding/subtracting: same exponent required first
02 LINEAR EQUATIONS & SLOPE▶

Slope Everything

  • m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁) = rise/run
  • Positive: rises left→right (up)
  • Negative: falls left→right (down)
  • Zero: horizontal line (y = k)
  • Undefined: vertical line (x = k)
  • Steeper line = larger absolute value of slope

Forms of a Line

  • Slope-intercept: y = mx + b (m=slope, b=y-int)
  • Standard form: Ax + By = C (A,B,C integers, A≥0)
  • Point-slope: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
  • Horizontal line: y = k (slope = 0)
  • Vertical line: x = k (slope = undefined)

Graphing Lines

  • Method 1: Plot y-intercept, use slope for next point
  • Method 2: Make an x-y table (choose 3 x-values)
  • Method 3: Find x-int (y=0) and y-int (x=0), connect
  • x-intercept: let y = 0, solve for x
  • y-intercept: let x = 0, solve for y

Parallel & Perpendicular

  • Parallel lines: SAME slope, different y-intercepts
  • Perpendicular lines: slopes are NEGATIVE RECIPROCALS
  • If m₁ = 2/3, perpendicular slope = −3/2
  • m₁ × m₂ = −1 for perpendicular
  • Coincident lines (same line): same slope AND same y-int
Writing equation from 2 points: 1) find slope using formula, 2) use point-slope form y−y₁=m(x−x₁), 3) simplify to slope-intercept
03 FUNCTIONS▶

Function Basics

  • Function: each input (x) maps to EXACTLY ONE output (y)
  • Vertical Line Test: if any vertical line crosses graph twice → NOT a function
  • Domain: all possible inputs (x-values)
  • Range: all possible outputs (y-values)
  • f(x) notation: f(x) means "output when input is x"
  • f(3) = plug 3 in for x, calculate

Types of Functions

  • Linear: f(x) = mx + b (constant rate of change)
  • Quadratic: f(x) = ax² + bx + c (parabola)
  • Exponential: f(x) = a·bˣ (growth/decay)
  • Absolute value: f(x) = |x| (V-shape)
  • Step function: staircase shape

Function Tables & Graphs

  • Table: look for pattern in outputs for equal input spacing
  • Linear table: constant difference in y-values
  • Quadratic table: constant second difference
  • Exponential table: constant ratio in y-values
  • Discrete: only certain input values (dots)
  • Continuous: all real number inputs (line)
04 SYSTEMS OF EQUATIONS▶

Types of Solutions

  • One solution: lines intersect at one point — consistent & independent
  • No solution: parallel lines (same slope, diff y-int) — inconsistent
  • Infinite solutions: same line — consistent & dependent

Substitution Method

  • 1. Isolate one variable in one equation
  • 2. Substitute that expression into the other equation
  • 3. Solve for remaining variable
  • 4. Substitute back to find the other variable
  • Best when: one variable already isolated or has coefficient 1

Elimination Method

  • 1. Align like terms vertically
  • 2. Multiply one or both equations so coefficients of one variable are opposites
  • 3. ADD the equations (variable cancels)
  • 4. Solve, then back-substitute
  • Best when: coefficients are already opposite or easily made so

Graphing Method

  • Put both equations in slope-intercept form
  • Graph both lines
  • Read intersection point (if any)
  • Good for visuals, less precise for non-integer solutions
05 POLYNOMIALS — ADD, SUBTRACT, MULTIPLY▶

Polynomial Vocabulary

  • Monomial: 1 term (4x², -7, 2xy)
  • Binomial: 2 terms (3x+5, x²-4)
  • Trinomial: 3 terms (x²+3x+2)
  • Polynomial: any number of terms
  • Degree: highest exponent in the polynomial
  • Leading coefficient: coefficient of highest degree term
  • Standard form: terms in descending degree order

Adding & Subtracting

  • Combine like terms only
  • Subtracting: distribute the negative to EVERY term
  • (3x²+2x+1) − (x²−5x+3) = 3x²+2x+1−x²+5x−3 = 2x²+7x−2

Multiplying Polynomials

  • Monomial × polynomial: distribute to each term
  • FOIL (binomial × binomial): First + Outer + Inner + Last
  • (x+3)(x+5) = x²+5x+3x+15 = x²+8x+15
  • Larger polynomials: distribute each term of first into second

Special Products

  • (a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
  • (a−b)² = a² − 2ab + b²
  • (a+b)(a−b) = a² − b² (difference of squares)
  • These patterns are worth memorizing for factoring!
06 FACTORING▶

Step 1 — Always: GCF

  • Find the greatest common factor of ALL terms
  • Factor it out front
  • 6x³ + 9x² = 3x²(2x + 3)
  • If leading coefficient is negative, factor out a negative
  • ALWAYS check GCF first before any other method

Factoring Trinomials (x²+bx+c)

  • Find two numbers that MULTIPLY to c AND ADD to b
  • x² + 7x + 12: find two numbers → 3 and 4 (3×4=12, 3+4=7)
  • Answer: (x+3)(x+4)
  • If c is positive and b is positive: both numbers positive
  • If c is positive and b is negative: both numbers negative
  • If c is negative: one positive, one negative

Factoring ax²+bx+c (a≠1)

  • Multiply a × c (AC method)
  • Find two numbers that multiply to ac and add to b
  • Rewrite bx as the sum of two terms
  • Factor by grouping
  • Example: 2x²+7x+3 → ac=6, factors of 6 that add to 7: 6&1 → 2x²+6x+x+3 → 2x(x+3)+1(x+3) → (2x+1)(x+3)

Difference of Squares & Perfect Squares

  • a²−b² = (a+b)(a−b)
  • x²−49 = (x+7)(x−7)
  • 4x²−25 = (2x+5)(2x−5)
  • Perfect square trinomial: a²+2ab+b² = (a+b)²
  • x²+10x+25 = (x+5)²
07 QUADRATIC EQUATIONS & PARABOLAS▶

Standard Form: ax²+bx+c

  • Parabola shape when graphed
  • a > 0: opens UP (minimum vertex)
  • a < 0: opens DOWN (maximum vertex)
  • |a| large: narrower parabola
  • |a| small: wider parabola
  • Axis of symmetry: x = −b/2a
  • Vertex x-coord: −b/2a; plug back in to get y

Solving Quadratics

  • By factoring: factor, set each factor = 0, solve
  • By square roots: ax² = c → x = ±√(c/a)
  • Completing the square: rewrite in (x+h)² = k form
  • Quadratic formula: works for ANY quadratic
  • Always set equation = 0 first

Quadratic Formula

  • x = [−b ± √(b²−4ac)] / 2a
  • Discriminant = b²−4ac
  • D > 0: TWO real solutions
  • D = 0: ONE real solution (repeated)
  • D < 0: NO real solutions (complex)
  • The ± gives you two solutions

Vertex Form

  • y = a(x−h)² + k
  • Vertex is at point (h, k)
  • Axis of symmetry: x = h
  • Easy to find vertex immediately
  • Converting to standard form: expand (x−h)²
Quadratic Formula: x = [−b ± √(b²−4ac)] / 2a  |  Vertex x: x = −b/2a
08 INEQUALITIES (LINEAR & SYSTEMS)▶

Linear Inequalities in Two Variables

  • Graph the boundary line (dashed for < >, solid for ≤ ≥)
  • Test a point (usually (0,0)) in inequality
  • If true: shade that region
  • If false: shade the opposite region
  • y > mx + b: shade ABOVE line
  • y < mx + b: shade BELOW line

Systems of Inequalities

  • Graph each inequality separately
  • Solution region: where all shading OVERLAPS
  • Each vertex of feasible region is a candidate for optimization
  • Test corner points for maximum/minimum values

01 SCIENTIFIC METHOD & LAB SKILLS▶

Scientific Method (Full Steps)

  • 1. Observation: notice something interesting
  • 2. Question: ask a testable question
  • 3. Background Research: gather existing knowledge
  • 4. Hypothesis: educated prediction (if...then...because)
  • 5. Experiment: test hypothesis with controlled conditions
  • 6. Data collection: record observations and measurements
  • 7. Analysis: look for patterns in data
  • 8. Conclusion: does data support hypothesis?
  • 9. Communication: share results

Variables Deep Dive

  • Independent (manipulated): what YOU change in the experiment
  • Dependent (responding): what CHANGES as a result; what you measure
  • Controlled variables (constants): everything KEPT THE SAME
  • Control GROUP: receives no treatment; used for comparison
  • Experimental group: receives the treatment being tested
  • X-axis on graph: independent variable
  • Y-axis on graph: dependent variable

SI Units & Measurement

  • Length: meter (m) — ruler, meterstick
  • Mass: kilogram (kg) — balance scale
  • Volume: liter (L) or cm³ — graduated cylinder
  • Temperature: Kelvin (K) or Celsius (°C)
  • Time: second (s)
  • Significant figures: meaningful digits in a measurement
  • Precision: closeness of repeated measurements
  • Accuracy: closeness to the TRUE value

Lab Safety & Tools

  • Always wear goggles with chemicals
  • Microscope: eyepiece × objective = total magnification
  • Graduated cylinder: read at bottom of meniscus
  • Thermometer: do not stir with it
  • Balance: zero before using; tare if needed
  • Data tables: label with units!
02 MATTER — PROPERTIES, STATES & CHANGES▶

Properties of Matter

  • Physical property: observed without changing composition (color, density, boiling point, hardness, magnetism)
  • Chemical property: describes ability to change into new substance (flammability, reactivity, toxicity)
  • Intensive property: does NOT depend on amount (density, temperature, color)
  • Extensive property: DEPENDS on amount (mass, volume, length)

States of Matter

  • Solid: definite shape, definite volume; particles vibrate in fixed positions; highly ordered
  • Liquid: no definite shape, definite volume; particles slide past each other; flows
  • Gas: no definite shape, no definite volume; particles far apart, move fast; fills container
  • Plasma: super-high energy ionized gas (4th state); stars, lightning, neon signs

Phase Changes

  • Melting: solid→liquid (absorbs heat; endothermic)
  • Freezing: liquid→solid (releases heat; exothermic)
  • Vaporization (evaporation/boiling): liquid→gas (absorbs)
  • Condensation: gas→liquid (releases)
  • Sublimation: solid→gas directly (dry ice, iodine)
  • Deposition: gas→solid directly (frost)
  • Phase changes occur at CONSTANT temperature

Physical vs Chemical Changes

  • Physical change: NO new substance; composition unchanged; often reversible
  • Examples physical: cutting, tearing, melting, dissolving, boiling, bending
  • Chemical change: NEW substance(s) formed; bonds broken AND formed; usually irreversible
  • Examples chemical: burning, rusting, cooking, digestion, fermentation, tarnishing
  • Signs of chemical change: gas produced (bubbles), precipitate forms, color change, energy change (heat/light), odor change

Mixtures

  • Homogeneous (solution): uniform throughout; can't see components (saltwater, air)
  • Heterogeneous: components visible; not uniform (salad, granite, blood)
  • Suspension: large particles settle out (muddy water)
  • Colloid: medium-sized particles don't settle (milk, fog, gelatin)
  • Separating: filtration, distillation, evaporation, chromatography

Density

  • D = m/V (density = mass ÷ volume)
  • Units: g/cm³ or g/mL
  • Water density: 1.0 g/cm³
  • Object floats if density < fluid density
  • Object sinks if density > fluid density
  • Density is an INTENSIVE property (doesn't change with amount)
03 ATOMIC THEORY & PERIODIC TABLE▶

History of Atomic Models

  • Dalton (1800s): indivisible sphere; atoms can't be created/destroyed
  • Thomson (1897): plum pudding model; discovered electron
  • Rutherford (1911): nuclear model; gold foil experiment; tiny dense nucleus
  • Bohr (1913): electrons in fixed circular orbits (energy levels)
  • Modern: electron cloud model; electrons in probability regions (orbitals)

Subatomic Particles

  • Proton: +1 charge, in nucleus, mass ≈ 1 amu
  • Neutron: 0 charge, in nucleus, mass ≈ 1 amu
  • Electron: −1 charge, electron cloud, mass ≈ 0 (negligible)
  • Atomic number = # protons (defines the element)
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons
  • Atomic mass on periodic table: weighted average of all isotopes

Isotopes & Ions

  • Isotope: same element (same protons), different # of neutrons
  • Carbon-12: 6 protons, 6 neutrons; Carbon-14: 6 protons, 8 neutrons
  • Isotopes have same chemical properties, slightly different mass
  • Ion: atom with unequal protons and electrons (has a charge)
  • Cation: lost electron(s) → positive charge (Na⁺, Ca²⁺)
  • Anion: gained electron(s) → negative charge (Cl⁻, O²⁻)

Periodic Table Organization

  • Period (horizontal row): elements with same # of electron shells
  • Group/Family (vertical column): same # of valence electrons, similar properties
  • Group 1 (alkali metals): very reactive, 1 valence e⁻
  • Group 2 (alkaline earth): 2 valence e⁻
  • Group 17 (halogens): 7 valence e⁻, very reactive nonmetals
  • Group 18 (noble gases): full valence shell, very UNREACTIVE
  • Transition metals: groups 3-12, middle of table

Metals, Nonmetals, Metalloids

  • Metals: left/center; shiny, malleable, ductile, conduct heat/electricity
  • Nonmetals: upper right; brittle (when solid), poor conductors
  • Metalloids (semimetals): staircase border; properties of both
  • Metalloids: B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te (used in semiconductors)
  • Periodic trends: atomic radius increases down/left; ionization energy increases up/right
04 CHEMICAL BONDING & REACTIONS▶

Chemical Bonding

  • Ionic bond: metal + nonmetal; transfer of electrons; forms crystals (NaCl)
  • Covalent bond: nonmetal + nonmetal; SHARING electrons
  • Metallic bond: metal + metal; "sea of electrons"; explains conductivity/malleability
  • Valence electrons: outermost electrons; determine bonding behavior
  • Octet rule: atoms want 8 valence electrons (stable)

Chemical Reactions

  • Reactants → Products (equation format)
  • Law of Conservation of Mass: matter cannot be created or destroyed
  • Balanced equation: same number of each atom on both sides
  • Coefficient: number in front of formula (multiplies everything)
  • Subscript: small number after element (can't change to balance)

Types of Chemical Reactions

  • Synthesis: A + B → AB (two things combine)
  • Decomposition: AB → A + B (one thing breaks apart)
  • Single replacement: A + BC → AC + B
  • Double replacement: AB + CD → AD + CB
  • Combustion: fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (+ energy)

Acids & Bases

  • Acid: pH < 7; tastes sour; produces H⁺ ions; HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃
  • Base: pH > 7; tastes bitter; slippery feel; produces OH⁻ ions; NaOH
  • Neutral: pH = 7 (pure water)
  • pH scale: 0-14; each unit is 10× change (logarithmic)
  • Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water
  • Indicators: change color based on pH (litmus, phenolphthalein)
05 FORCES, MOTION & NEWTON'S LAWS▶

Newton's 3 Laws (Complete)

  • 1st Law (Inertia): object at rest stays at rest; object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity — unless acted on by NET force
  • 2nd Law: F = ma (force equals mass times acceleration); larger mass needs more force for same acceleration
  • 3rd Law: for every action there is an equal and opposite REACTION; forces act on DIFFERENT objects

Motion Definitions

  • Distance: total path length (scalar)
  • Displacement: shortest distance from start to end + direction (vector)
  • Speed: distance/time (scalar; no direction)
  • Velocity: displacement/time + direction (vector)
  • Acceleration: change in velocity / time (Δv/t)
  • Deceleration: negative acceleration (slowing down)

Motion Formulas

  • Speed: v = d/t
  • Acceleration: a = (vf − vi) / t
  • Force: F = ma
  • Weight: W = mg (g = 9.8 m/s² on Earth)
  • Momentum: p = mv
  • Work: W = Fd (force × distance)
  • Power: P = W/t (work/time)

Types of Forces

  • Gravity: pulls toward center of Earth (9.8 m/s²)
  • Normal force: perpendicular to surface; surface pushes back
  • Friction: opposes motion; static (not moving) > kinetic (moving)
  • Applied force: direct push or pull
  • Tension: pulling force through a rope/string
  • Air resistance (drag): opposes motion through air
  • Net force: vector sum of ALL forces on an object

Free Fall & Projectile Motion

  • Free fall: only gravity acts on object (neglect air resistance)
  • All objects fall at same rate regardless of mass (Galileo)
  • Acceleration due to gravity: 9.8 m/s² downward
  • Projectile: horizontal and vertical motion are INDEPENDENT
  • Horizontal velocity: constant (no horizontal force)
  • Vertical: accelerates due to gravity

Momentum & Impulse

  • Momentum: p = mv (mass × velocity)
  • Law of Conservation of Momentum: total momentum before = total after in collision
  • Elastic collision: KE conserved (objects bounce)
  • Inelastic collision: objects stick together
  • Impulse = force × time = change in momentum

06 ENERGY — TYPES, CONSERVATION & TRANSFER▶

Kinetic & Potential Energy

  • Kinetic energy (KE): energy of motion — KE = ½mv²
  • Faster speed → exponentially more KE (squared relationship)
  • Gravitational PE: energy stored due to height — PE = mgh
  • Elastic PE: energy stored in stretched/compressed spring
  • Chemical PE: energy stored in molecular bonds
  • At top of roller coaster: max PE, min KE
  • At bottom: min PE, max KE

Other Forms of Energy

  • Thermal (heat): random kinetic energy of particles
  • Electrical: movement of electrons through conductor
  • Sound: mechanical waves (longitudinal); needs medium
  • Light (radiant): electromagnetic waves; travels through vacuum
  • Nuclear: stored in atomic nucleus; fission (split) or fusion (combine)

Law of Conservation of Energy

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only converted
  • Total energy of closed system remains constant
  • Useful energy + "wasted" energy (usually heat) = input energy
  • Efficiency = (useful energy out / total energy in) × 100%

Heat Transfer Methods

  • Conduction: direct contact; best in solids; metals conduct well
  • Convection: fluid movement (liquid/gas); hot fluid rises, cool sinks — creates currents
  • Radiation: electromagnetic waves; NO medium needed; how sun heats Earth
  • Heat always flows from HOT to COLD
  • Insulation: slows heat transfer
07 WAVES, SOUND & ELECTROMAGNETIC SPECTRUM▶

Wave Properties

  • Wavelength (λ): distance between two identical consecutive points (crest to crest)
  • Frequency (f): number of waves passing a point per second; unit = Hz
  • Amplitude: maximum displacement from rest; related to ENERGY
  • Wave speed: v = λ × f (speed = wavelength × frequency)
  • Period: time for one complete cycle; T = 1/f

Types of Waves

  • Transverse wave: particles vibrate PERPENDICULAR to wave direction (light, water surface)
  • Longitudinal (compressional): particles vibrate PARALLEL to wave direction (sound)
  • Mechanical waves: need a medium (sound, water waves, seismic)
  • Electromagnetic waves: do NOT need medium (light, radio, X-rays)

Wave Behaviors

  • Reflection: wave bounces off surface; angle in = angle out
  • Refraction: wave bends when entering new medium (different speed)
  • Diffraction: wave bends around obstacles or through openings
  • Interference: constructive (amplitudes add) or destructive (amplitudes cancel)
  • Absorption: wave energy taken in by material

Electromagnetic Spectrum

  • Low to high frequency: Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X-ray → Gamma
  • Long to short wavelength: same order
  • All EM waves travel at c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum
  • Higher frequency = higher energy = shorter wavelength
  • Visible light: ROY G BIV (red = longest λ, lowest f; violet = shortest λ, highest f)

Sound

  • Longitudinal mechanical wave; needs medium
  • Travels fastest in solids, slowest in gases
  • Speed in air: ≈340 m/s at room temp
  • Pitch: related to frequency (high freq = high pitch)
  • Loudness: related to amplitude
  • Doppler effect: frequency shifts when source or observer moves
08 EARTH — LAYERS, PLATE TECTONICS & ROCKS▶

Earth's Interior Layers

  • Crust: thinnest layer; oceanic (thinner, denser, basalt) and continental (thicker, less dense, granite)
  • Mantle: largest layer; upper mantle = lithosphere + asthenosphere; hot semi-solid rock flows slowly
  • Outer core: liquid iron and nickel; convection creates Earth's magnetic field
  • Inner core: solid iron and nickel; extreme pressure; hottest (≈5000°C)

Plate Tectonics

  • Lithosphere (crust + rigid upper mantle) broken into tectonic plates
  • Plates float on asthenosphere; driven by convection currents in mantle
  • Convergent boundary: plates collide → subduction, mountains, volcanoes, earthquakes
  • Divergent boundary: plates move apart → rift valleys, mid-ocean ridges, seafloor spreading
  • Transform boundary: plates slide past → earthquakes (San Andreas Fault)

Earthquakes

  • Focus (hypocenter): where earthquake starts underground
  • Epicenter: point on surface directly above focus
  • P-waves (primary): fastest; compressional; travel through anything
  • S-waves (secondary): slower; shear; cannot travel through liquids
  • Surface waves: slowest; cause most damage
  • Richter scale: measures magnitude (each unit = 10× more energy)
  • 3 seismograph stations needed to locate epicenter (triangulation)

Volcanoes

  • Form at convergent and divergent boundaries AND hotspots
  • Magma: molten rock underground; lava: at surface
  • Shield volcano: gently sloping; basaltic lava; Hawaiian type
  • Composite (stratovolcano): steep sides; explosive; Mt. St. Helens
  • Cinder cone: small, steep; made of volcanic fragments

Rock Cycle

  • Igneous: formed from cooled magma or lava; intrusive (slow, large crystals) or extrusive (fast, small/no crystals)
  • Sedimentary: compaction and cementation of sediments; often contains fossils; layers (strata)
  • Metamorphic: existing rock changed by heat AND pressure (without melting)
  • ALL rocks can transform into any other type via the rock cycle
09 ATMOSPHERE, WEATHER & CLIMATE▶

Atmosphere Layers

  • Troposphere: 0–12 km; ALL weather happens here; temp decreases with altitude
  • Stratosphere: 12–50 km; ozone layer (blocks UV); temp increases with altitude
  • Mesosphere: 50–80 km; coldest layer; meteors burn up here
  • Thermosphere: 80–700 km; auroras; ISS orbits here; very hot but low density
  • Exosphere: outer edge; satellites; merges with space

Water Cycle

  • Evaporation: water surface → water vapor (solar energy drives this)
  • Transpiration: plants release water vapor (together = evapotranspiration)
  • Condensation: water vapor → liquid (cloud formation)
  • Precipitation: water falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
  • Runoff: flows over land into streams/rivers
  • Infiltration: soaks into ground → groundwater
  • Accumulation/Collection: collects in bodies of water

Weather Factors

  • Air pressure: high pressure = clear skies; low pressure = storms
  • Air mass: large body of air with uniform temp & humidity
  • Cold front: cold air pushing under warm → thunderstorms, quick change
  • Warm front: warm air sliding over cold → gentle rain, gradual change
  • Humidity: amount of water vapor in air; relative humidity = % of max
  • Dew point: temperature at which air becomes saturated

Climate & Climate Change

  • Climate: average weather patterns over 30+ years in a region
  • Factors affecting climate: latitude, altitude, proximity to water, ocean currents, prevailing winds
  • Greenhouse effect: natural process; CO₂, H₂O, CH₄ trap heat in atmosphere
  • Enhanced greenhouse effect: human CO₂ emissions intensify warming
  • Effects: rising temps, melting ice caps, sea level rise, extreme weather, ecosystem disruption
  • El Niño: periodic warming of Pacific Ocean; affects global weather

01 CELL STRUCTURE & ORGANELLES▶

Cell Theory (3 Tenets)

  • 1. All living things are made of one or more cells
  • 2. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life
  • 3. All cells come from pre-existing cells (cell division)
  • Robert Hooke: first saw cells (cork, 1665)
  • Schleiden (plants) + Schwann (animals) + Virchow (cells from cells) = Cell Theory

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic

  • Prokaryotic: NO membrane-bound nucleus; DNA in nucleoid region; bacteria & archaea; smaller; simpler
  • Eukaryotic: HAS true membrane-bound nucleus; plants, animals, fungi, protists; larger; complex organelles
  • Both have: DNA, ribosomes, cell membrane, cytoplasm

Animal Cell Organelles

  • Nucleus: contains DNA; controls cell activities; has nuclear envelope
  • Nucleolus: inside nucleus; makes rRNA for ribosomes
  • Mitochondria: cellular respiration → ATP energy; has own DNA; "powerhouse"
  • Ribosomes: protein synthesis; found on rough ER or free in cytoplasm
  • Rough ER: studded with ribosomes; processes/transports proteins
  • Smooth ER: no ribosomes; makes lipids; detoxifies
  • Golgi apparatus: packages, modifies, ships proteins; "post office"
  • Lysosome: contains digestive enzymes; breaks down waste/pathogens
  • Vacuole: storage (small in animal cells)
  • Centrosome/Centrioles: organize spindle fibers during cell division
  • Cell membrane: phospholipid bilayer; selectively permeable

Plant-Only Organelles

  • Cell wall: cellulose; rigid outer layer; provides structure and protection
  • Chloroplast: photosynthesis; has thylakoids (grana) and stroma; contains chlorophyll
  • Large central vacuole: takes up most of cell; stores water; gives turgor pressure
  • Plasmodesmata: channels connecting adjacent plant cells
  • Plants have ALL animal organelles PLUS these (no centrioles, no lysosomes)
02 CELL MEMBRANE & TRANSPORT▶

Cell Membrane Structure

  • Phospholipid bilayer: two layers of phospholipids
  • Hydrophilic heads face water (inside and outside of cell)
  • Hydrophobic tails face each other (middle)
  • Embedded proteins: transport channels, receptors, enzymes
  • Fluid mosaic model: membrane is flexible; components can move
  • Selectively permeable: controls what enters and exits

Passive Transport (no energy needed)

  • Diffusion: movement from HIGH to LOW concentration (with gradient)
  • Osmosis: diffusion of WATER through a selectively permeable membrane
  • Facilitated diffusion: with proteins helping; still HIGH to LOW; no energy
  • Equilibrium: equal concentration on both sides; diffusion continues but no net movement

Active Transport (energy/ATP needed)

  • Moves materials AGAINST concentration gradient (LOW to HIGH)
  • Requires protein pumps AND ATP energy
  • Endocytosis: cell engulfs material (phagocytosis = solids; pinocytosis = liquids)
  • Exocytosis: cell releases material by fusing vesicle with membrane

Osmosis in Cells

  • Hypotonic solution: solute concentration LOWER than cell → water enters cell → cell swells/bursts (lysis)
  • Hypertonic solution: solute concentration HIGHER than cell → water leaves cell → cell shrinks (crenation/plasmolysis)
  • Isotonic solution: equal concentrations → no net water movement → cell stays normal
  • Plant cells in hypotonic: turgid (firm — good)
  • Plant cells in hypertonic: wilts (plasmolysis)
03 PHOTOSYNTHESIS & CELLULAR RESPIRATION▶

Photosynthesis

  • Overall equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Location: chloroplasts
  • Chlorophyll: absorbs light energy (green wavelengths reflected)
  • Light-dependent reactions: in thylakoid membranes; split water; produce ATP, NADPH, O₂
  • Calvin cycle (light-independent): in stroma; uses ATP+NADPH to make glucose from CO₂
  • Inputs: CO₂ (from air), H₂O (from roots), light energy
  • Outputs: glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), oxygen (released to air)

Cellular Respiration

  • Overall equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP
  • Purpose: convert glucose to usable ATP energy
  • Location: mitochondria (and cytoplasm for glycolysis)
  • Glycolysis: cytoplasm; glucose → 2 pyruvate; produces 2 ATP; no O₂ needed
  • Krebs cycle: mitochondrial matrix; releases CO₂; produces electron carriers
  • Electron transport chain: inner mitochondrial membrane; produces ~32-34 ATP; requires O₂
  • Total ATP: ~36-38 ATP per glucose molecule

Fermentation (Anaerobic)

  • When O₂ is not available
  • Lactic acid fermentation: in muscle cells; glucose → lactic acid; causes muscle burning/fatigue
  • Alcoholic fermentation: in yeast; glucose → ethanol + CO₂
  • Only produces 2 ATP (much less than aerobic)

Photosynthesis vs Respiration

  • They are essentially REVERSE reactions
  • Photosynthesis: uses CO₂ + H₂O + light → makes glucose + O₂
  • Respiration: uses glucose + O₂ → makes CO₂ + H₂O + energy
  • Autotrophs (plants): do BOTH photosynthesis and respiration
  • Heterotrophs (animals): only cellular respiration
04 CELL DIVISION — MITOSIS & MEIOSIS▶

The Cell Cycle

  • Interphase: G1 (growth) → S (DNA synthesis/replication) → G2 (prep for division)
  • Mitotic phase: PMAT + cytokinesis
  • DNA replication in S phase: each chromosome duplicated → two sister chromatids joined at centromere
  • Cell checkpoints: ensure cell is ready to divide; prevents errors
  • Cancer: cell checkpoint failures; uncontrolled division

Mitosis Phases (PMAT)

  • Prophase: chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes; nucleolus disappears; spindle forms; nuclear envelope breaks down
  • Metaphase: chromosomes line up at cell's equator (metaphase plate); spindle attaches to centromeres
  • Anaphase: sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles; cell elongates
  • Telophase: nuclear envelopes reform around each set; chromosomes decondense
  • Cytokinesis: cytoplasm divides (cleavage furrow in animals; cell plate in plants)

Mitosis Results

  • Produces 2 genetically IDENTICAL daughter cells
  • Same chromosome number as parent cell (diploid 2n → 2n)
  • Human body cells: 46 chromosomes (23 pairs)
  • Purpose: growth, tissue repair, asexual reproduction

Meiosis Overview

  • Produces sex cells (gametes): sperm and eggs
  • Two rounds of division: Meiosis I + Meiosis II
  • Result: 4 genetically UNIQUE haploid cells (n)
  • Haploid: half the chromosome number (23 in humans)
  • Crossing over (synapsis) in Prophase I: homologous chromosomes exchange DNA segments → genetic variation
  • Independent assortment: homologs separate randomly → more variation

Mitosis vs Meiosis Comparison

  • Mitosis: 1 division, 2 cells, diploid, identical, body cells
  • Meiosis: 2 divisions, 4 cells, haploid, unique, sex cells
  • Both start with DNA replication in S phase
  • Fertilization: sperm (n=23) + egg (n=23) → zygote (2n=46)
05 DNA, GENES & PROTEIN SYNTHESIS▶

DNA Structure

  • Double helix: two polynucleotide strands wound around each other
  • Nucleotide structure: deoxyribose sugar + phosphate group + nitrogenous base
  • 4 bases: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C)
  • Base pairing rules: A–T (2 hydrogen bonds); G–C (3 hydrogen bonds)
  • Antiparallel strands: run in opposite directions (5'→3' and 3'→5')
  • Discovered by Watson & Crick in 1953 using Franklin's X-ray crystallography

DNA Replication

  • Semiconservative: each new DNA has one original + one new strand
  • Helicase: unwinds and separates the double helix
  • DNA polymerase: adds new complementary nucleotides
  • Occurs during S phase of interphase
  • Occurs in the NUCLEUS

Transcription (DNA → RNA)

  • Occurs in the NUCLEUS
  • Template strand of DNA read by RNA polymerase
  • Produces messenger RNA (mRNA)
  • RNA uses Uracil (U) instead of Thymine (T)
  • mRNA carries genetic code out of nucleus to ribosome

Translation (RNA → Protein)

  • Occurs at RIBOSOMES
  • Codon: sequence of 3 mRNA bases coding for one amino acid
  • tRNA: carries amino acids; anticodon matches mRNA codon
  • Start codon: AUG (codes for methionine)
  • Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (end translation)
  • Polypeptide chain → folds into protein

Mutations

  • Point mutation: one base changed (substitution)
  • Frameshift: insertion or deletion of bases; shifts reading frame; often devastating
  • Chromosomal mutation: large segment deleted, duplicated, inverted, or translocated
  • Somatic mutation: body cell; not passed to offspring
  • Germline mutation: in sperm or egg; CAN be inherited
  • Mutagens: UV light, X-rays, chemicals, viruses
06 GENETICS & HEREDITY — MENDEL & BEYOND▶

Mendelian Genetics

  • Gregor Mendel: studied peas; Father of Genetics
  • Law of Segregation: two alleles for each trait separate during gamete formation; offspring get one from each parent
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes sort independently of each other
  • Dominant allele (capital): expressed even when only one copy present
  • Recessive allele (lowercase): ONLY expressed when two copies present (homozygous recessive)

Genotype & Phenotype

  • Alleles: different versions of a gene
  • Genotype: actual allele combination (TT, Tt, tt)
  • Phenotype: observable physical trait (tall vs short)
  • Homozygous dominant: TT (shows dominant trait)
  • Heterozygous: Tt (shows dominant trait — carrier)
  • Homozygous recessive: tt (shows recessive trait)

Punnett Squares

  • Monohybrid (one trait): 2×2 grid
  • Tt × Tt: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt = 3:1 phenotype ratio
  • Tt × tt (testcross): 1 Tt : 1 tt = 1:1 ratio
  • Dihybrid (two traits): 4×4 grid; 9:3:3:1 ratio
  • Probability: each cross is independent; multiply probabilities

Non-Mendelian Inheritance

  • Codominance: BOTH alleles fully expressed; neither dominant (AB blood type; roan cattle)
  • Incomplete dominance: blended phenotype (RR=red, WW=white, RW=pink)
  • Multiple alleles: more than 2 alleles in population (ABO blood type: A, B, O)
  • Polygenic inheritance: multiple genes control one trait (height, skin color, eye color)
  • Environmental influence: phenotype affected by environment (temperature, nutrition)

Sex-Linked Traits

  • Sex chromosomes: XX = female; XY = male
  • X-linked traits: carried on X chromosome (color blindness, hemophilia)
  • Males are more often affected by X-linked recessive (only one X)
  • Carrier female: heterozygous; has one copy but shows normal phenotype
  • X-linked recessive: skips generations; more common in males

Blood Types (ABO)

  • Type A: A antigens on RBC; A or O alleles (AA or AO)
  • Type B: B antigens; BB or BO
  • Type AB: both A and B antigens; AB (codominant)
  • Type O: no antigens; OO (recessive)
  • Universal donor: O negative
  • Universal recipient: AB positive
  • Rh factor: + or − is separate from ABO
07 EVOLUTION & NATURAL SELECTION▶

Darwin's Theory of Evolution

  • Natural selection: mechanism of evolution; "survival of the fittest"
  • Fitness: ability to survive AND reproduce in a given environment
  • Variation: individuals in a population differ from each other
  • Heritability: traits can be passed to offspring
  • Differential reproduction: individuals with favorable traits reproduce more
  • Over time: favorable traits become more common in population

Types of Natural Selection

  • Directional selection: one extreme phenotype favored → shifts population mean
  • Stabilizing selection: intermediate phenotype favored → reduces variation
  • Disruptive selection: both extremes favored → may lead to speciation
  • Sexual selection: traits favored by mate choice (peacock feathers)
  • Artificial selection: humans selectively breed organisms (dog breeds, crops)

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil record: shows change in species over time; transitional fossils
  • Comparative anatomy: homologous structures (same origin, different function — human arm, whale flipper, bat wing)
  • Analogous structures: different origin, same function (convergent evolution)
  • Vestigial structures: reduced/functionless structures (human coccyx, whale pelvis)
  • Molecular evidence: DNA/protein similarities reflect evolutionary relationships
  • Biogeography: geographic distribution of species (Galápagos finches)
  • Direct observation: bacteria evolving antibiotic resistance

Speciation & Mechanisms

  • Speciation: formation of new species
  • Reproductive isolation: populations can no longer interbreed
  • Allopatric speciation: geographic barrier separates populations
  • Genetic drift: random changes in allele frequencies (small populations)
  • Founder effect: small group colonizes new area; limited genetic variation
  • Bottleneck effect: population severely reduced; loss of variation
  • Gene flow: movement of alleles between populations
08 ECOLOGY — ECOSYSTEMS & INTERACTIONS▶

Ecological Levels of Organization

  • Atom → Molecule → Organelle → Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism
  • Population: all individuals of one species in an area
  • Community: all populations of different species in an area
  • Ecosystem: community + all abiotic (non-living) factors
  • Biome: large ecosystem defined by climate and vegetation
  • Biosphere: all life on Earth + environments

Energy Flow in Ecosystems

  • Producers (autotrophs): make own food via photosynthesis (plants, algae, cyanobacteria)
  • Primary consumers (herbivores): eat producers
  • Secondary consumers: eat primary consumers
  • Tertiary consumers: eat secondary consumers (apex predators)
  • Decomposers: bacteria, fungi; break down dead matter; recycle nutrients
  • Detritivores: eat dead organic matter (earthworms, millipedes)
  • 10% rule: only 10% of energy transfers to next trophic level; 90% lost as heat

Symbiotic Relationships

  • Mutualism: BOTH species benefit (+/+) — clownfish & anemone, bees & flowers
  • Commensalism: one benefits, other unaffected (+/0) — barnacles on whale
  • Parasitism: parasite benefits, host harmed (+/−) — tapeworm in host
  • Predation: predator kills and eats prey (+/−) — hawk & rabbit
  • Competition: both species harmed (−/−) — two species competing for same food
  • Amensalism: one harmed, other unaffected (−/0)

Population Dynamics

  • Carrying capacity (K): maximum population an environment can support
  • Limiting factors: food, water, space, disease, predators
  • Biotic factors: living (predators, disease, competition)
  • Abiotic factors: non-living (temperature, water, sunlight, pH)
  • Logistic growth: S-curve; levels off at carrying capacity
  • Exponential growth: J-curve; unrestricted growth (no limiting factors)

Biogeochemical Cycles

  • Carbon cycle: photosynthesis removes CO₂; respiration/combustion adds CO₂; fossil fuels release stored carbon
  • Nitrogen cycle: N₂ → nitrates via nitrogen-fixing bacteria → plants → animals → decomposers → N₂
  • Water cycle: evaporation → transpiration → condensation → precipitation → runoff → infiltration
  • Phosphorus cycle: weathering of rocks → soil → plants → animals → decomposers → soil

Biomes of the World

  • Tropical rainforest: highest biodiversity; hot, wet year-round
  • Temperate deciduous forest: four seasons; deciduous trees
  • Grassland (savanna): few trees; seasonal rainfall; large herbivores
  • Desert: <25cm rain/year; extreme temperatures; low biodiversity
  • Tundra: treeless; permafrost; very cold; brief summer
  • Taiga (boreal forest): coniferous trees; cold winters; moose, bears
  • Aquatic: freshwater (lakes, rivers, wetlands) and marine (ocean, coral reef, estuary)
