01 NUMBER SYSTEMS & OPERATIONS ▶

Real Number Types

  • Natural: 1, 2, 3... (counting numbers)
  • Whole: 0, 1, 2, 3...
  • Integers: ...-2,-1,0,1,2...
  • Rational: any a/b form (b≠0)
  • Irrational: non-repeating decimals (π, √2)

Integer Operations Rules

  • Pos × Pos = Positive
  • Neg × Neg = Positive
  • Pos × Neg = Negative
  • Adding same signs → add, keep sign
  • Adding diff signs → subtract, keep bigger sign

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

  • Parentheses first
  • Exponents
  • Multiply / Divide (L→R)
  • Add / Subtract (L→R)
  • Never skip steps

Absolute Value

  • |x| = distance from zero
  • Always ≥ 0
  • |-5| = 5 and |5| = 5
  • |-a| = |a|
02 FRACTIONS, DECIMALS & PERCENTS ▶

Fraction Operations

  • Add/Sub: need common denominator (LCD)
  • Multiply: multiply tops × tops, bottoms × bottoms
  • Divide: keep, change, flip (KCF)
  • Mixed → improper: (whole × denom + num)/denom

Percent Conversions

  • % → decimal: divide by 100 (move . left 2)
  • Decimal → %: multiply by 100 (move . right 2)
  • Fraction → %: divide then × 100
  • % of number: convert then multiply

Ratio & Proportion

  • Ratio: comparison of two quantities
  • Proportion: two equal ratios a/b = c/d
  • Cross multiply to solve: ad = bc
  • Unit rate: ratio with denominator = 1
% change = (new - old) / old × 100
Simple Interest = P × r × t (principal × rate × time)
Sale price = original × (1 - discount rate)
03 VARIABLES, EXPRESSIONS & EQUATIONS ▶

Algebraic Terms

  • Variable: letter representing unknown
  • Coefficient: number in front of variable
  • Constant: a fixed number
  • Term: number, variable, or combination
  • Like terms: same variable AND same exponent

Combining Like Terms

  • Only combine terms that are alike
  • Add/subtract coefficients only
  • Variable part stays the same
  • 3x + 5x = 8x ✓
  • 3x + 5y ≠ 8xy ✗

Distributive Property

  • a(b + c) = ab + ac
  • Multiply EVERYTHING inside
  • Works with subtraction too
  • 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12
  • -2(x - 5) = -2x + 10

Solving One-Step Equations

  • Goal: get variable alone
  • Use inverse operations
  • Whatever you do to one side, do to both
  • x + 5 = 12 → x = 7
  • 3x = 21 → x = 7
Two-step equations: 1) undo addition/subtraction first, 2) then undo multiplication/division
Variables on both sides: move variables to one side first using inverse ops, then solve
04 INEQUALITIES ▶

Inequality Symbols

  • < less than (open circle)
  • > greater than (open circle)
  • ≤ less than or equal (closed circle)
  • ≥ greater than or equal (closed circle)

Solving Inequalities

  • Same steps as equations
  • FLIP the sign when multiplying or dividing by a NEGATIVE
  • -2x < 8 → x > -4 (sign flipped!)
  • Graph on number line

Graphing on Number Line

  • Open circle: < or >
  • Closed (filled) circle: ≤ or ≥
  • Shade left for less than
  • Shade right for greater than

Probability

⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — ONE & TWO-STEP EQUATIONS ▶
Think of an equation as a balance scale. Whatever weight you add or remove from one side, you MUST do the same to the other side or it tips. Your ONLY goal: get the variable completely alone. Undo operations in the reverse order they were applied — undo + and − first, then × and ÷.
INVERSE OPERATIONS  |  + ↔ −    × ↔ ÷    x² ↔ √
// ONE-STEP — ADDITION/SUBTRACTION
EXAMPLE A
x + 9 = 17
  1. 9 is added to x. Undo it by subtracting 9 from BOTH sides.
    x + 9 − 9 = 17 − 9
    The +9 and −9 cancel on the left
  2. Left side simplifies to just x.
    x = 8
  3. CHECK: plug 8 back in. 8 + 9 = 17 ✓
x = 8
EXAMPLE B
x − 7 = 4
  1. 7 is subtracted. Undo by adding 7 to both sides.
    x − 7 + 7 = 4 + 7
  2. Simplify.
    x = 11
  3. CHECK: 11 − 7 = 4 ✓
x = 11
// ONE-STEP — MULTIPLICATION/DIVISION
EXAMPLE C
4x = 28
  1. x is being multiplied by 4. Undo by dividing both sides by 4.
    4x / 4 = 28 / 4
    The 4s cancel on the left
  2. x = 7
  3. CHECK: 4 × 7 = 28 ✓
x = 7
EXAMPLE D — fraction coefficient
x/3 = 5
  1. x is divided by 3. Undo by multiplying both sides by 3.
    x/3 × 3 = 5 × 3
  2. x = 15
  3. CHECK: 15/3 = 5 ✓
x = 15
// TWO-STEP EQUATIONS — always undo + / − FIRST, then × / ÷
EXAMPLE E
3x − 4 = 11
  1. Undo the −4 first. Add 4 to both sides.
    3x − 4 + 4 = 11 + 4
    3x = 15
  2. Now undo the ×3. Divide both sides by 3.
    3x / 3 = 15 / 3
    x = 5
  3. CHECK: 3(5) − 4 = 15 − 4 = 11 ✓
x = 5
EXAMPLE F — negative result
2x + 10 = 4
  1. Subtract 10 from both sides.
    2x = 4 − 10 = −6
    It's ok for x to be negative!
  2. Divide both sides by 2.
    x = −3
  3. CHECK: 2(−3) + 10 = −6 + 10 = 4 ✓
x = −3
// VARIABLES ON BOTH SIDES — collect variables to one side first
EXAMPLE G
5x + 2 = 3x + 10
  1. Move variables to left: subtract 3x from both sides.
    5x − 3x + 2 = 10
    2x + 2 = 10
  2. Subtract 2.
    2x = 8
  3. Divide by 2.
    x = 4
  4. CHECK: 5(4)+2 = 22  =  3(4)+10 = 22 ✓
x = 4
EXAMPLE H — needs distributing first
2(x + 3) = x + 9
  1. Distribute the 2 first.
    2x + 6 = x + 9
  2. Subtract x from both sides.
    x + 6 = 9
  3. Subtract 6.
    x = 3
  4. CHECK: 2(3+3) = 12  =  3+9 = 12 ✓
x = 3
COMMON MISTAKE: Forgetting to distribute before collecting like terms. Anytime you see a number directly outside parentheses like 3(x+2), you MUST multiply through before doing anything else.
⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — FRACTION OPERATIONS ▶
ADD/SUB: need LCD  |  MULTIPLY: straight across  |  DIVIDE: Keep·Change·Flip
The #1 rule: you can ONLY add or subtract fractions when the denominators are the same. Think of it like combining apples — you can't add 1 third-apple and 1 quarter-apple without cutting them into same-size pieces first (that's the LCD).
// HOW TO FIND THE LCD (LEAST COMMON DENOMINATOR)
FINDING LCD — method: list multiples
Find LCD of 4 and 6
  1. List multiples of 4:  4, 8, 12, 16, 20...
  2. List multiples of 6:  6, 12, 18, 24...
  3. Find the SMALLEST number that appears in BOTH lists.
    LCD = 12
LCD = 12
// ADDING FRACTIONS
EXAMPLE A — different denominators
1/3 + 1/4
  1. Find LCD of 3 and 4.
    LCD = 12
  2. Convert 1/3: multiply top and bottom by 4.
    1×4 / 3×4 = 4/12
  3. Convert 1/4: multiply top and bottom by 3.
    1×3 / 4×3 = 3/12
  4. Add numerators, KEEP denominator.
    4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12
7/12
EXAMPLE B — mixed numbers
2½ + 1¾
  1. Convert to improper fractions.
    2½ = 5/2    1¾ = 7/4
    Formula: (whole × denom + num) / denom
  2. LCD of 2 and 4 is 4. Convert 5/2.
    5/2 = 10/4
  3. Add: 10/4 + 7/4 = 17/4
  4. Convert back: 17 ÷ 4 = 4 remainder 1.
    = 4¼
4¼
// SUBTRACTING FRACTIONS
EXAMPLE C
5/6 − 1/4
  1. LCD of 6 and 4 = 12.
  2. Convert: 5/6 = 10/12   1/4 = 3/12
  3. Subtract numerators.
    10/12 − 3/12 = 7/12
7/12
EXAMPLE D — borrowing with mixed numbers
3 − 1⅓
  1. Convert 3 to thirds:  3 = 9/3
  2. Convert 1⅓ to improper:  4/3
  3. Subtract:  9/3 − 4/3 = 5/3 = 1⅔
1⅔
// MULTIPLYING FRACTIONS — straight across, no LCD needed
EXAMPLE E
3/4 × 2/5
  1. Multiply numerators:  3 × 2 = 6
  2. Multiply denominators:  4 × 5 = 20
  3. Result: 6/20. Simplify by dividing by GCF (2).
    6/20 = 3/10
3/10
EXAMPLE F — cross-cancel trick
4/9 × 3/8
  1. Cross-cancel BEFORE multiplying: 4 and 8 share factor 4; 3 and 9 share factor 3.
    ⁴⁄₉ × ³⁄₈ → ¹⁄₃ × ¹⁄₂
    Divide diagonally to simplify early
  2. Multiply simplified fractions.
    1/3 × 1/2 = 1/6
1/6
// DIVIDING FRACTIONS — Keep · Change · Flip (KCF)
EXAMPLE G
(2/3) ÷ (4/5)
  1. Keep the first fraction:  2/3
  2. Change ÷ to ×
  3. Flip the second fraction (reciprocal):  4/5 → 5/4
  4. Multiply straight across.
    2/3 × 5/4 = 10/12 = 5/6
5/6
EXAMPLE H — whole number divisor
(3/4) ÷ 2
  1. Write 2 as a fraction:  2 = 2/1
  2. KCF: Keep 3/4, Change to ×, Flip 2/1 → 1/2.
    3/4 × 1/2 = 3/8
3/8
COMMON MISTAKES: (1) Adding denominators — NEVER do 1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7. (2) Forgetting to simplify final answer. (3) Flipping the WRONG fraction when dividing — always flip the SECOND one (the divisor).
⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — PERCENT PROBLEMS ▶
Part = (Percent / 100) × Whole     % Change = (Change / Original) × 100
Every percent problem is one of THREE types: find the PART, find the WHOLE, or find the PERCENT. The IS/OF trick: "IS" = part (numerator), "OF" = whole (denominator). So "15 is what % of 60?" → IS = 15, OF = 60, % = 15/60 × 100 = 25%.
// TYPE 1 — FIND THE PART
EXAMPLE A
What is 35% of 80?
  1. Convert % to decimal: divide by 100.
    35% = 35/100 = 0.35
  2. Multiply decimal by the whole number.
    0.35 × 80 = 28
28
EXAMPLE B — tax/tip
Meal costs $24. 8% tax. Total tax?
  1. Convert: 8% = 0.08
  2. Multiply: 0.08 × 24 = $1.92
  3. Total = $24 + $1.92 = $25.92
$25.92
// TYPE 2 — FIND THE PERCENT
EXAMPLE C
15 is what % of 60?
  1. Write as fraction: Part/Whole.
    15/60
  2. Divide: 15 ÷ 60 = 0.25
  3. Multiply by 100 to get %.
    0.25 × 100 = 25%
25%
EXAMPLE D — test score
Got 34 out of 40 correct. What %?
  1. 34/40 = 0.85
  2. 0.85 × 100 = 85%
85%
// TYPE 3 — FIND THE WHOLE
EXAMPLE E
18 is 30% of what number?
  1. Set up equation: Part = % × Whole.
    18 = 0.30 × W
  2. Divide both sides by 0.30.
    W = 18 / 0.30 = 60
60
EXAMPLE F — sale price
Item on 25% off sale costs $45. Original price?
  1. Paying 75% of original (100% − 25%).
    0.75 × P = 45
  2. P = 45 / 0.75 = $60
$60
// PERCENT CHANGE — increase or decrease
EXAMPLES — always divide by the ORIGINAL
Price: $50 → $65. % increase?
  1. Change = 65 − 50 = 15
  2. % change = 15/50 × 100 = 30% increase
Crowd: 200 → 150. % decrease?
  1. Change = 200 − 150 = 50
  2. % change = 50/200 × 100 = 25% decrease
⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — INEQUALITIES & GRAPHING ▶
< or > → open circle      ≤ or ≥ → closed (filled) circle
Solve inequalities EXACTLY like equations — with ONE critical exception: multiply or divide by a NEGATIVE → FLIP the sign. Why? Because −1 < 2 is true, but multiply both sides by −1 and you get 1 > −2 (sign flipped to stay true). The number line never lies — always check with a test value.
// STEP-BY-STEP PROCESS
EXAMPLE A — no flip needed
2x + 5 < 13
  1. Subtract 5 from both sides.
    2x < 8
  2. Divide by 2 (positive, no flip).
    x < 4
  3. Graph: open circle at 4, shade LEFT.
    Open circle because x cannot equal 4
  4. CHECK with x = 0: 2(0)+5=5 < 13 ✓
x < 4
EXAMPLE B — FLIP required
−3x + 6 ≥ −9
  1. Subtract 6 from both sides.
    −3x ≥ −15
  2. Divide by −3. SIGN FLIPS ≥ becomes ≤.
    x ≤ 5
    Dividing by NEGATIVE → flip direction
  3. Graph: closed circle at 5, shade LEFT.
    Closed circle because x CAN equal 5
  4. CHECK with x = 0: −3(0)+6=6 ≥ −9 ✓ (0 ≤ 5) ✓
x ≤ 5
EXAMPLE C — variables on both sides
5x − 3 > 2x + 9
  1. Subtract 2x from both sides.
    3x − 3 > 9
  2. Add 3 to both sides.
    3x > 12
  3. Divide by 3 (positive, no flip).
    x > 4
  4. Graph: open circle at 4, shade RIGHT.
x > 4
EXAMPLE D — compound inequality
−2 ≤ 2x + 4 < 10
  1. Subtract 4 from ALL three parts.
    −6 ≤ 2x < 6
  2. Divide all three parts by 2.
    −3 ≤ x < 3
  3. Graph: closed circle at −3, open circle at 3, shade BETWEEN.
−3 ≤ x < 3
⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — AREA, PERIMETER & PYTHAGOREAN THEOREM ▶
Area inside shape  |  Perimeter = outer edge total  |  a² + b² = c²
// AREA FORMULAS — applied
RECTANGLE — A = l × w
l = 12 cm, w = 7 cm
  1. Multiply length × width.
    A = 12 × 7 = 84 cm²
  2. Perimeter = 2(l + w) = 2(12+7) = 38 cm
A = 84 cm², P = 38 cm
TRIANGLE — A = ½ × b × h
base = 10 m, height = 6 m
  1. The height must be PERPENDICULAR to the base (may not be a side).
  2. A = ½ × 10 × 6 = ½ × 60 = 30 m²
A = 30 m²
CIRCLE — A = πr², C = 2πr
radius = 5 ft
  1. Area: π × 5² = π × 25 ≈ 78.5 ft²
  2. Circumference: 2 × π × 5 ≈ 31.4 ft
  3. r = radius (half the diameter). If given diameter, divide by 2 first!
A ≈ 78.5 ft², C ≈ 31.4 ft
TRAPEZOID — A = ½(b₁+b₂)h
b₁=8, b₂=5, h=4
  1. Add the two parallel bases: 8 + 5 = 13
  2. Multiply by height then divide by 2.
    A = ½ × 13 × 4 = 26
A = 26 units²
// PYTHAGOREAN THEOREM — RIGHT TRIANGLES ONLY
a² + b² = c²    c = hypotenuse (longest side, across from right angle)
The hypotenuse is ALWAYS c. It's the longest side and it's always across from the 90° angle. Memorize these triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17. Any multiple works: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, 10-24-26.
EXAMPLE — find hypotenuse
legs: a = 9, b = 12, find c
  1. Square both legs.
    9² = 81    12² = 144
  2. Add.
    81 + 144 = 225
  3. Square root.
    c = √225 = 15
c = 15
EXAMPLE — find a missing leg
a = 8, c = 17, find b
  1. Rearrange for the missing leg.
    b² = c² − a²
  2. Substitute.
    b² = 17² − 8² = 289 − 64 = 225
  3. Square root.
    b = √225 = 15
b = 15
COMMON MISTAKE: Adding the hypotenuse to find a leg: a² + c² ≠ b². When finding a leg, SUBTRACT: b² = c² − a². The hypotenuse is always what you subtract FROM.
⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — PROBABILITY & STATISTICS ▶
P(event) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes    Mean = sum / count
// PROBABILITY
EXAMPLE A — simple probability
Bag has 3 red, 5 blue, 2 green marbles. P(red)?
  1. Count favorable outcomes: 3 red
  2. Count total outcomes: 3+5+2 = 10
  3. P(red) = 3/10 = 0.3 = 30%
3/10 or 30%
EXAMPLE B — complement rule
P(NOT rolling a 6) on a die?
  1. P(6) = 1/6
  2. Complement: P(not 6) = 1 − P(6)
    = 1 − 1/6 = 5/6
5/6
// MEAN, MEDIAN, MODE, RANGE
DATA SET: 4, 7, 7, 9, 13, 15, 15, 15, 20
  1. MEAN (average): add all, divide by count.
    (4+7+7+9+13+15+15+15+20)/9 = 105/9 ≈ 11.67
  2. MEDIAN (middle when sorted): data has 9 values, middle is 5th.
    4, 7, 7, 9, [13], 15, 15, 15, 20 → median = 13
  1. MODE (most frequent): 15 appears 3 times.
    Mode = 15
  2. RANGE: max − min.
    20 − 4 = 16
Even number of data values? Median = average of the TWO middle numbers. Outliers affect the MEAN the most, not median or mode — that's why median is used for home prices instead of average!

  • Associative: (a+b)+c = a+(b+c)

  • Identity: a+0=a, a×1=a

Exponent Rules

  • Quotient: xᵃ ÷ xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ
  • Power: (xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ
  • Zero: x⁰ = 1 (x ≠ 0)
  • Negative: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ

Scientific Notation

  • Form: a × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ a < 10
  • Large numbers: positive n
  • Small numbers: negative n
  • Multiplying: multiply a's, add exponents
  • Dividing: divide a's, subtract exponents
02 LINEAR EQUATIONS & FUNCTIONS ▶

Slope

  • m = rise/run = (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁)
  • Positive slope: goes up left→right
  • Negative slope: goes down left→right
  • Zero slope: horizontal line
  • Undefined slope: vertical line

Forms of Linear Equations

  • Slope-intercept: y = mx + b
  • Standard form: Ax + By = C
  • Point-slope: y-y₁ = m(x-x₁)
  • m = slope, b = y-intercept

Graphing a Line

  • 1. Find y-intercept (b) — plot on y-axis
  • 2. Use slope (rise/run) to find next point
  • 3. Draw line through points
  • x-int: set y=0, solve for x
  • y-int: set x=0, solve for y

Functions

  • Function: each input → exactly one output
  • Vertical Line Test: if line crosses twice → NOT a function
  • Domain: all valid inputs (x values)
  • Range: all valid outputs (y values)
Parallel lines have the SAME slope (different y-intercepts)
Perpendicular lines have slopes that are NEGATIVE RECIPROCALS (m₁ × m₂ = -1)
03 SYSTEMS OF EQUATIONS ▶

Solving by Graphing

  • Graph both equations
  • Solution = intersection point
  • One solution: lines cross once
  • No solution: parallel lines
  • Infinite solutions: same line

Substitution Method

  • 1. Solve one equation for one variable
  • 2. Substitute into other equation
  • 3. Solve for remaining variable
  • 4. Plug back in to find other variable

Elimination Method

  • 1. Line up equations
  • 2. Add or subtract to eliminate a variable
  • 3. May need to multiply first
  • 4. Solve, then substitute back
04 POLYNOMIALS ▶

Polynomial Vocabulary

  • Monomial: one term (3x²)
  • Binomial: two terms (x+5)
  • Trinomial: three terms (x²+3x+2)
  • Degree: highest exponent
  • Leading coefficient: front term's coefficient

Adding & Subtracting

  • Combine like terms only
  • Subtracting: distribute the negative first
  • (2x²+3x)+(x²-x) = 3x²+2x

Multiplying Polynomials

  • FOIL: First, Outer, Inner, Last (binomials)
  • For longer: distribute each term
  • (x+2)(x+3) = x²+5x+6
  • Special: (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²
  • Special: (a+b)(a-b) = a²-b²
05 FACTORING ▶

GCF Factoring

  • Find greatest common factor of all terms
  • Factor it out front
  • 6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3)
  • Always check GCF first!

Factoring Trinomials (x²+bx+c)

  • Find two numbers that multiply to c AND add to b
  • x²+5x+6 = (x+2)(x+3)
  • 2 × 3 = 6 and 2+3 = 5 ✓

Difference of Squares

  • a²-b² = (a+b)(a-b)
  • x²-25 = (x+5)(x-5)
  • Both terms must be perfect squares
  • Signs must be opposite
06 QUADRATIC EQUATIONS ▶

Standard Form

  • ax² + bx + c = 0
  • Parabola shape when graphed
  • Vertex: highest/lowest point
  • Axis of symmetry: x = -b/2a

Solving Methods

  • Factoring (when factorable)
  • Square roots (no bx term)
  • Completing the square
  • Quadratic formula (always works)
Quadratic Formula: x = (-b ± √(b²-4ac)) / 2a
Discriminant (b²-4ac): positive = 2 real solutions, zero = 1 solution, negative = no real solutions
⊕ HOW TO SOLVE — Step-by-Step Methods ▶
// METHOD 1 — SLOPE & LINEAR EQUATIONS
m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)    y = mx + b
Slope = "rise over run" — how much you go UP (or down) divided by how much you go RIGHT. If the line goes up → positive slope. Goes down → negative slope. Flat line → slope = 0. Vertical → undefined (can't divide by 0). The b in y = mx + b is where the line CROSSES the y-axis (set x = 0).
EXAMPLE — find slope from two points
Points: (2, 3) and (6, 11)
  1. Label points: (x₁, y₁) = (2,3) and (x₂, y₂) = (6,11)
  2. Plug into slope formula.
    m = (11 − 3) / (6 − 2)
  3. Simplify.
    m = 8 / 4 = 2
m = 2
EXAMPLE — write equation from slope + point
m = 3, passes through (1, 5)
  1. Use point-slope: y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
    y − 5 = 3(x − 1)
  2. Distribute.
    y − 5 = 3x − 3
  3. Solve for y.
    y = 3x + 2
y = 3x + 2
// METHOD 2 — SYSTEMS OF EQUATIONS (SUBSTITUTION)
Substitution: solve one equation for one variable, then PLUG that into the other equation. You'll get a number, then substitute back to find the other variable. Always check by plugging BOTH values into BOTH original equations.
WORKED EXAMPLE
y = 2x + 1    and    3x + y = 16
  1. Since y = 2x + 1, substitute (2x + 1) for y in the second equation.
    3x + (2x + 1) = 16
  2. Combine like terms.
    5x + 1 = 16
  3. Subtract 1, divide by 5.
    5x = 15 → x = 3
  4. Substitute x = 3 back into y = 2x + 1.
    y = 2(3) + 1 = 7
x = 3, y = 7
// METHOD 2B — ELIMINATION
Elimination: line up equations and add/subtract them to make one variable disappear. If coefficients don't match, multiply one (or both) equations by a number first to create matching coefficients.
WORKED EXAMPLE
2x + 3y = 12    and    2x − y = 4
  1. Subtract the second equation from the first to eliminate 2x.
    (2x+3y) − (2x−y) = 12 − 4
    4y = 8
  2. Solve for y.
    y = 2
  3. Substitute y = 2 into either equation.
    2x − 2 = 4 → 2x = 6 → x = 3
x = 3, y = 2
// METHOD 3 — FOIL & POLYNOMIAL MULTIPLICATION
(a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd
FOIL stands for: First · Outer · Inner · Last. Draw arrows connecting each pair — First×First, then reach across (outer), then the inside pair (inner), then Last×Last. Always combine like terms at the end.
WORKED EXAMPLE
(x + 3)(x + 5)
  1. FIRST: x × x
    x²
  2. OUTER: x × 5
    5x
  3. INNER: 3 × x
    3x
  4. LAST: 3 × 5
    15
  5. Add and combine like terms.
    x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15
x² + 8x + 15
// METHOD 4 — FACTORING TRINOMIALS
x² + bx + c  →  find two numbers: multiply to c, add to b
Reverse of FOIL. For x² + bx + c: find two numbers that MULTIPLY to c and ADD to b. List factor pairs of c and check which pair adds to b. If c is negative, the two numbers have opposite signs.
EXAMPLE — positive terms
x² + 7x + 12
  1. Need two numbers that × to 12 and + to 7.
    Pairs of 12: (1,12), (2,6), (3,4)
  2. Which pair adds to 7?
    3 + 4 = 7 ✓
  3. Write the factored form.
    (x + 3)(x + 4)
(x + 3)(x + 4)
EXAMPLE — negative constant
x² + 2x − 15
  1. Need × to −15, + to 2.
    Opposite signs since c is negative
  2. Pairs: (5,−3): 5×−3=−15 ✓ and 5+(−3)=2 ✓
  3. Factored form.
    (x + 5)(x − 3)
(x + 5)(x − 3)
// METHOD 5 — QUADRATIC FORMULA
x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a    for ax² + bx + c = 0
Use this when factoring won't work. Step one: get the equation to equal ZERO. Step two: identify a, b, c. Step three: plug into the formula. The ± means you get TWO answers — one with + and one with −. The discriminant b²−4ac tells you how many solutions: positive → 2, zero → 1, negative → none.
WORKED EXAMPLE
x² − 5x + 6 = 0
  1. Identify a = 1, b = −5, c = 6
  2. Plug into formula.
    x = (−(−5) ± √((−5)² − 4·1·6)) / (2·1)
  3. Simplify inside the square root.
    x = (5 ± √(25 − 24)) / 2 = (5 ± √1) / 2
  4. Two answers: (5+1)/2 = 3   and   (5−1)/2 = 2
x = 3 or x = 2
// METHOD 6 — EXPONENT RULES APPLIED
Same base and multiplying? ADD exponents. Same base and dividing? SUBTRACT exponents. Power to a power? MULTIPLY exponents. Anything to the zero power = 1. Negative exponent = flip it to the denominator.
QUICK REFERENCE WITH EXAMPLES
xᵃ · xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ   →   x³ · x⁵ = x⁸
xᵃ / xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ   →   x⁷ / x² = x⁵
(xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ   →   (x³)⁴ = x¹²
x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ   →   x⁻³ = 1/x³

01 SCIENTIFIC METHOD & MEASUREMENT ▶

Scientific Method Steps

  • 1. Observation / Question
  • 2. Background Research
  • 3. Hypothesis (if-then statement)
  • 4. Experiment Design
  • 5. Data Collection & Analysis
  • 6. Conclusion
  • 7. Communicate results

Variables

  • Independent (manipulated): what you CHANGE
  • Dependent (responding): what you MEASURE
  • Control: what stays the SAME
  • Control group: no treatment (comparison)
  • Experimental group: receives treatment

Measurement & SI Units

  • Length: meter (m)
  • Mass: kilogram (kg)
  • Time: second (s)
  • Temperature: Kelvin (K) or Celsius (°C)
  • Volume: liter (L) or m³

Accuracy vs. Precision

  • Accuracy: how close to true value
  • Precision: how consistent results are
  • Can be precise but not accurate
  • Goal: both accurate AND precise
  • Error = experimental - accepted
02 MATTER & ATOMIC STRUCTURE ▶

States of Matter

  • Solid: fixed shape, fixed volume, tightly packed
  • Liquid: no fixed shape, fixed volume, flows
  • Gas: no fixed shape, no fixed volume, spreads out
  • Plasma: high-energy ionized gas (4th state)

Phase Changes

  • Melting: solid → liquid (absorbs heat)
  • Freezing: liquid → solid (releases heat)
  • Evaporation/Boiling: liquid → gas (absorbs)
  • Condensation: gas → liquid (releases)
  • Sublimation: solid → gas directly
  • Deposition: gas → solid directly

Atomic Structure

  • Proton: positive charge, in nucleus
  • Neutron: no charge, in nucleus
  • Electron: negative charge, in electron cloud
  • Atomic number = # protons
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons
  • Neutral atom: # protons = # electrons

Periodic Table

  • Period (row): same # of electron shells
  • Group (column): same valence electrons
  • Metals: left side, conduct electricity
  • Nonmetals: right side, poor conductors
  • Metalloids: staircase, properties of both
Isotopes: same # protons, different # neutrons. Same element, different mass number.
Ions: Cation = lost electrons (positive). Anion = gained electrons (negative).
03 PHYSICAL vs CHEMICAL CHANGES ▶

Physical Changes

  • NO new substance formed
  • Chemical identity stays same
  • Usually reversible
  • Examples: cutting, melting, dissolving, bending, boiling water

Chemical Changes

  • NEW substance(s) formed
  • Chemical bonds broken and formed
  • Usually NOT easily reversible
  • Examples: burning, rusting, cooking, digestion

Signs of Chemical Change

  • Gas production (bubbles)
  • Precipitate forms (solid in liquid)
  • Color change
  • Heat/light produced or absorbed
  • Odor change
04 FORCES & MOTION ▶

Newton's Laws of Motion

  • 1st (Inertia): objects resist changes in motion — stay at rest or keep moving unless net force acts
  • 2nd: F = ma (force = mass × acceleration)
  • 3rd: For every action, equal and opposite reaction

Motion Equations

  • Speed = distance / time (v = d/t)
  • Velocity = speed WITH direction
  • Acceleration = Δv / t = (vf - vi) / t
  • Momentum = mass × velocity (p = mv)

Types of Forces

  • Gravity: pulls toward Earth (9.8 m/s²)
  • Friction: opposes motion (kinetic/static)
  • Normal force: perpendicular to surface
  • Applied force: push or pull
  • Net force: sum of all forces

Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces

  • Balanced: net force = 0, no change in motion
  • Unbalanced: net force ≠ 0, causes acceleration
  • Free body diagrams show all forces
  • Forces are vectors (have direction)
v = d/t  |  a = Δv/t  |  F = ma  |  W = mg
05 ENERGY ▶

Types of Energy

  • Kinetic: energy of motion (KE = ½mv²)
  • Potential: stored energy
  • Gravitational PE = mgh
  • Elastic PE: springs/rubber bands
  • Chemical: in bonds (food, batteries)
  • Thermal: heat (random particle motion)
  • Electromagnetic: light, radio waves

Energy Transformations

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed
  • Law of Conservation of Energy
  • Roller coaster: PE↔KE constantly
  • Light bulb: electrical → light + thermal
  • Plant: light → chemical (photosynthesis)

Heat Transfer

  • Conduction: direct contact (spoon in soup)
  • Convection: fluid movement (boiling water)
  • Radiation: electromagnetic waves (sun)
  • Heat flows from hot → cold
06 WAVES, SOUND & LIGHT ▶

Wave Properties

  • Wavelength (λ): distance between crests
  • Frequency (f): # of waves per second (Hz)
  • Amplitude: height of wave (energy!)
  • Speed = wavelength × frequency (v = λf)
  • Transverse: vibrate ⊥ to direction
  • Longitudinal: vibrate ∥ to direction (sound)

Electromagnetic Spectrum

  • Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → UV → X-ray → Gamma
  • All travel at speed of light (c = 3×10⁸ m/s)
  • Higher frequency = higher energy
  • Higher frequency = shorter wavelength

Light Behavior

  • Reflection: bounces off surface
  • Refraction: bends when changing medium
  • Absorption: energy taken in
  • Transmission: passes through
  • Visible light: ROY G BIV (red→violet)
07 EARTH SCIENCE — PLATE TECTONICS ▶

Earth's Layers

  • Crust: thin outer layer (oceanic & continental)
  • Mantle: largest layer, rock that flows slowly
  • Outer core: LIQUID iron & nickel
  • Inner core: SOLID iron & nickel (hottest)

Plate Boundaries

  • Convergent: plates collide → mountains, volcanoes, earthquakes
  • Divergent: plates separate → rift valleys, mid-ocean ridges
  • Transform: plates slide past → earthquakes (San Andreas)

Volcanoes & Earthquakes

  • Earthquake: caused by plate movement along faults
  • Epicenter: point on surface above focus
  • P-waves: primary, faster, push-pull
  • S-waves: secondary, slower, side-to-side
  • Richter scale: measures magnitude

Rock Cycle

  • Igneous: formed from cooled magma/lava
  • Sedimentary: formed from compressed sediments
  • Metamorphic: changed by heat and pressure
  • All can transform into each other
08 WEATHER & CLIMATE ▶

Weather vs Climate

  • Weather: short-term atmospheric conditions (today)
  • Climate: average weather over 30+ years
  • Microclimate: local climate variation

Water Cycle

  • Evaporation: water to vapor (sun)
  • Transpiration: plants release water vapor
  • Condensation: vapor → clouds
  • Precipitation: rain, snow, sleet, hail
  • Runoff / Infiltration / Collection

Atmosphere Layers

  • Troposphere: weather happens here (0-12km)
  • Stratosphere: ozone layer (12-50km)
  • Mesosphere: meteors burn up
  • Thermosphere: auroras, ISS orbits
  • Exosphere: edge of space

Climate Change

  • Greenhouse effect: CO₂/CH₄ trap heat
  • Greenhouse gases: CO₂, methane, water vapor
  • Effects: rising temps, sea level rise, extreme weather
  • Natural vs human-caused (anthropogenic)

01 CELL STRUCTURE & FUNCTION ▶

Cell Theory

  • All living things are made of cells
  • Cell = basic unit of life
  • All cells come from pre-existing cells
  • Two types: Prokaryotic & Eukaryotic

Animal Cell Organelles

  • Nucleus: "brain" — stores DNA
  • Mitochondria: powerhouse — makes ATP energy
  • Ribosome: makes proteins
  • ER (rough): has ribosomes, makes proteins
  • ER (smooth): makes lipids, detox
  • Golgi apparatus: packages and ships proteins
  • Lysosome: digests waste
  • Cell membrane: controls what enters/exits

Plant-Only Organelles

  • Cell wall: rigid support (cellulose)
  • Chloroplast: photosynthesis
  • Central vacuole: large, stores water, gives structure
  • Plasmodesmata: connect plant cells

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic

  • Prokaryotic: no membrane-bound nucleus (bacteria, archaea)
  • Eukaryotic: has true nucleus (plants, animals, fungi, protists)
  • Prokaryotic = simpler, smaller
  • Both have DNA, ribosomes, cell membrane
02 PHOTOSYNTHESIS & CELLULAR RESPIRATION ▶

Photosynthesis

  • Where: chloroplasts (thylakoids + stroma)
  • Inputs: CO₂ + H₂O + light energy
  • Outputs: glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) + O₂
  • Light reactions: in thylakoids, capture light
  • Calvin cycle: in stroma, makes glucose

Cellular Respiration

  • Where: mitochondria (and cytoplasm)
  • Inputs: glucose + O₂
  • Outputs: CO₂ + H₂O + ATP (energy!)
  • Glycolysis: cytoplasm, breaks glucose
  • Krebs cycle + ETC: mitochondria, makes ATP
Photosynthesis equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Cellular respiration equation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP energy
They are OPPOSITE processes! What one uses, the other makes. This cycles matter through ecosystems.
03 CELL DIVISION — MITOSIS & MEIOSIS ▶

Mitosis (Somatic Cells)

  • Purpose: growth, repair, asexual reproduction
  • Result: 2 identical daughter cells
  • Same chromosome number as parent (diploid)
  • PMAT: Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase
  • Followed by cytokinesis (cell splits)

Phases of Mitosis

  • Interphase: DNA duplicates (not mitosis itself)
  • Prophase: chromosomes visible, spindle forms
  • Metaphase: chromosomes line up in middle
  • Anaphase: chromatids pulled to poles
  • Telophase: nuclear envelope reforms

Meiosis (Sex Cells)

  • Purpose: make gametes (sperm/egg)
  • Result: 4 genetically unique cells
  • Half chromosomes (haploid)
  • Two rounds: Meiosis I + Meiosis II
  • Crossing over creates genetic variation
Mitosis = 2 identical copies (BODY cells). Meiosis = 4 unique half-copies (SEX cells). Remember: Meiosis has MORE cells = MORE unique.
04 DNA, GENES & PROTEIN SYNTHESIS ▶

DNA Structure

  • Double helix shape
  • Made of nucleotides
  • Each nucleotide: sugar + phosphate + base
  • Bases: A, T, G, C
  • Base pairs: A-T and G-C always
  • Discovered by Watson & Crick (1953)

Protein Synthesis

  • Transcription: DNA → mRNA (in nucleus)
  • mRNA carries genetic code to ribosome
  • Translation: mRNA → protein (at ribosome)
  • tRNA brings amino acids
  • Codon: 3 mRNA bases = 1 amino acid

Mutations

  • Change in DNA sequence
  • Point mutation: one base changed
  • Insertion/deletion: bases added or removed
  • Can be harmful, neutral, or beneficial
  • Caused by: radiation, chemicals, errors
05 GENETICS & HEREDITY ▶

Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation: alleles separate during gamete formation
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes assort independently
  • Dominant allele: expressed even when one copy present (capital letter)
  • Recessive: only expressed when two copies (lowercase)

Genotype & Phenotype

  • Genotype: actual allele combination (TT, Tt, tt)
  • Phenotype: physical appearance/trait
  • Homozygous: same alleles (TT or tt)
  • Heterozygous: different alleles (Tt)

Punnett Squares

  • Grid to predict offspring ratios
  • Monohybrid: one trait (2×2)
  • Dihybrid: two traits (4×4)
  • Tt × Tt → 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt
  • 3:1 phenotype ratio for monohybrid

Non-Mendelian Inheritance

  • Codominance: both alleles expressed (AB blood type)
  • Incomplete dominance: blending (red+white=pink)
  • Sex-linked: traits on X or Y chromosome
  • Polygenic: multiple genes control trait (height, skin color)
06 EVOLUTION & NATURAL SELECTION ▶

Darwin's Theory

  • Natural selection: survival of the fittest
  • Variation exists in populations
  • Heritable traits can be passed on
  • Organisms with advantageous traits reproduce more
  • Over time, populations change

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil record: shows change over time
  • Comparative anatomy: homologous structures
  • DNA evidence: similar genes = common ancestor
  • Biogeography: distribution of species
  • Vestigial structures: leftover useless parts

Key Vocabulary

  • Adaptation: trait that improves survival
  • Speciation: formation of new species
  • Common ancestor: shared evolutionary origin
  • Genetic drift: random allele frequency changes
  • Gene flow: alleles moving between populations
07 ECOLOGY & ECOSYSTEMS ▶

Ecological Levels

  • Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere
  • Population: same species, same area
  • Community: all species in area
  • Ecosystem: biotic + abiotic factors

Energy Flow

  • Producers (autotrophs): make own food (plants)
  • Primary consumers: eat producers (herbivores)
  • Secondary consumers: eat primary consumers
  • Tertiary consumers: top predators
  • Decomposers: break down dead matter
  • 10% rule: only 10% energy passes to next level

Symbiosis

  • Mutualism: both benefit (+/+)
  • Commensalism: one benefits, other neutral (+/0)
  • Parasitism: one benefits, one harmed (+/-)
  • Predation: predator kills prey
  • Competition: both species harmed (-/-)

Biogeochemical Cycles

  • Carbon cycle: CO₂ ↔ organic matter
  • Nitrogen cycle: N₂ → nitrates → organisms
  • Water cycle: evaporation → precipitation
  • Phosphorus cycle: rocks → soil → organisms
